First-timers guide

Your first Orlando trip, planned properly.

Overwhelmed by Disney, Universal, tickets, hotels and timings? This guide walks you through your very first Orlando holiday step by step—so you can stop guessing and start getting excited.

For UK & European travellers
First-timers & “it’s been years” trips
Family & couples friendly

Step 1

Before you book anything

Before you lock in flights, hotels or tickets, you need three things clear: when you’re going, how long you’re staying, and what kind of trip you actually want.

Decide the “feel” of your trip

  • Is this a once-in-a-lifetime blowout, or a “we’ll be back again” kind of trip?
  • Do you want full park days, or a slower mix of parks, pool and shopping?
  • Are you travelling with young kids, teens, adults only, or a mix?
  • Is your priority Disney, Universal, “do it all”, or “keep it simple”?
First big family trip Adults-only Disney Teens & thrill rides Multi-generational

How long should you stay?

  • 7–10 nights: A “taster” trip. You’ll need to prioritise.
  • 10–14 nights: The sweet spot for most first-timers.
  • 14+ nights: Slower pace, more rest days, more exploring.
First-timer tip: however long you think you need, add at least one full non-park day. Jet lag, heat and walking catch up with everyone.
Step 2

When to go (and what to expect)

Orlando is open all year, but crowds, heat and prices change a lot depending on when you go. There’s no “perfect” time—just the best time for your priorities.

Popular times for first-timers

  • Easter & summer holidays: busy, hot, but great atmosphere and long hours.
  • October half-term: Halloween events, slightly cooler, still popular.
  • Christmas & New Year: magical but extremely busy and premium pricing.

If you can be flexible

  • Look at shoulder seasons for slightly lower crowds and nicer weather.
  • Check school holidays, US holidays and major events before you commit.
  • Decide what matters more: lower crowds, lower cost, or specific seasonal events.
First-timer tip: don’t chase the “perfect” week. Pick a realistic window, then plan smartly around crowds and heat.
Step 3

Where to stay: on-site vs off-site

Your hotel choice affects your budget, transport, park access and overall stress levels. There’s no one “best” option—just what fits your family, your budget and your priorities.

Staying on-site (Disney or Universal)

  • Closer to the parks, often with included transport.
  • Immersive theming and “bubble” feeling.
  • Early entry and other on-site perks (varies by hotel and year).
  • Usually higher cost per night, especially for larger families.

Staying off-site (villas & hotels)

  • More space for the money—great for families and groups.
  • Flexibility with food, pool time and non-park days.
  • Will usually need a car or to plan transport carefully.
  • Less “in the bubble”, but often more relaxed.
First-timer tip: decide if you’re a “we’ll be in the parks all day” family or a “we want space and downtime” family. That answer usually decides your hotel.
Step 4

Tickets, budget & realistic costs

Orlando isn’t a cheap holiday, but it doesn’t have to be chaotic either. A clear ticket plan and a realistic budget will stop the “are we doing this right?” panic before it starts.

Start with your non-negotiables

  • Is Disney a must-do, a nice-to-have, or not this trip?
  • Is Universal (and the big coasters) essential for your group?
  • Do you want water parks, or are you happy with hotel pool days?
  • Are you planning shopping days and off-park exploring?

Build your budget around:

  • Flights and accommodation (your biggest fixed costs).
  • Park tickets (Disney, Universal, others) based on your priorities.
  • Food & drink—both in-park and off-site.
  • Transport (car hire, parking, fuel, or transfers).
  • Extras: souvenirs, photos, special experiences.
First-timer tip: decide what you’re happy to spend before you start adding “just one more thing”. Orlando will always offer you more—you don’t have to say yes to everything.
Step 5

How to structure your park days

A good plan doesn’t mean scheduling every minute. It means knowing which days are parks, which are rest, and which are flexible—so you don’t burn out halfway through.

A simple first-timer rhythm

  • Alternate heavy park days with lighter or non-park days where possible.
  • Plan early starts for key parks, then allow for earlier finishes.
  • Keep at least one “do whatever we feel like” day in the middle of the trip.
  • Don’t try to “do it all” in one visit—leave room to enjoy being there.

Use tools, not pressure

  • Use our itineraries as a guide, not a rulebook.
  • Make a short list of “must-do” rides and experiences for each park.
  • Accept that you won’t see everything—and that’s okay.
  • Build in time for photos, snacks and just soaking it all in.
First-timer tip: your future self will thank you for every rest break you plan in advance. Tired feet and hot kids don’t care how much the tickets cost.
Step 6

First-timer mistakes to avoid

Everyone makes little mistakes on their first Orlando trip—but some are easy to dodge once you know they’re coming. Here are the big ones to watch out for.

Common first-timer mistakes

  • Trying to “do everything” in one trip and ending up exhausted.
  • Not planning any proper rest days or slower mornings.
  • Underestimating walking distances, heat and queue times.
  • Leaving dining, transport or key bookings until the last minute.

What to do instead

  • Pick your non-negotiables and let the rest be a bonus.
  • Plan “breathing space” into your schedule on purpose.
  • Wear comfortable shoes and be realistic about pace.
  • Use this guide, then dive into our Disney, Universal and hotel pages for detail.
First-timer tip: your trip doesn’t have to be perfect to be unforgettable. The goal is happy memories, not a completed checklist.

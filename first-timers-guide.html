First‑Timers Guide to Orlando

Planning your first Orlando holiday without losing the magic (or your mind)

Right, put the kettle on, and let's sort this out together

Welcome to Orlando

One of the first things I tell all my clients is this: remember to breathe. You’re meant to be going on holiday. So take a breath, relax your shoulders, and let’s make this whole thing feel a lot less overwhelming.

Planning your first Orlando holiday can feel a bit like standing in the middle of a theme park map thinking, “Where on earth do we start?” Disney, Universal, SeaWorld, waterparks, tickets, hotels, dining, transport… it’s a lot.

That’s where I come in. I don’t want you sat there stressed with twenty tabs open and a notebook full of scribbles. I want you feeling like you’ve popped the kettle on, had a chat with a friend who knows Orlando inside out, and suddenly it all makes sense.

I’ve helped so many families, couples and friends plan their first Orlando adventure — from “we’ve no idea what we’re doing” to “we can’t believe how smooth that was”. This guide is everything I’d talk you through if we were sat together over a cuppa: honest advice, real expectations, and the little tips that make a big difference.

Orlando isn’t just a holiday. It’s an experience. Let’s make sure yours is a good one.

Step 1 — When to go (for UK visitors)

When to go

Most of us in the UK don’t have the luxury of picking any random week of the year. School holidays rule the calendar, and Orlando knows it.

The busiest times of the year
These are the most popular times for UK families:

Easter
Summer holidays
Christmas & New Year

Yes, they’re busy — but they’re also brilliant. Longer park hours, more entertainment, a buzzing atmosphere, and that “everyone’s in it together” feeling. With the right plan, you can absolutely have an amazing first trip in these periods without feeling like you’ve spent the whole time in a queue.

If you’re more flexible
Lovely times to visit include:

Late April to mid‑May
Mid‑September to mid‑November

You’ll usually find a nice balance of warmth, value and slightly calmer crowds. I’ll never tell you “don’t go” at peak times. My job is to help you make it work, not make you feel bad about your dates.

Step 2 — How long to stay

How long to stay

For UK visitors, Orlando isn’t a quick week in the sun — it’s a proper adventure.

Most of my clients stay 14 to 21 nights, and honestly, that’s where Orlando really shines. It gives you time to:

Spread out your park days
Build in pool days and lazy mornings
Go back to favourite parks
Enjoy evenings at Disney Springs, CityWalk or International Drive
Actually breathe, instead of marching from ride to ride

You don’t have to do everything (and you won’t — no one does), but a longer stay means you don’t feel like you’re on a military operation. You get the fun and the memories, without the burnout.

Step 3 — Understanding tickets

Understanding tickets (UK‑friendly, no jargon)

One of the biggest advantages we have in the UK is the ticket options. They’re different to what you see on American sites, and they’re usually much better value.

Walt Disney World – 14‑Day Magic Ticket
You get:

All 4 Disney theme parks
Both Disney water parks
Park hopping
Memory Maker
Mini golf
Parking only if staying at a Disney resort

Universal Orlando – All Parks Ticket
You get:

Universal Studios
Islands of Adventure
Epic Universe
Volcano Bay

Important: from 20 Oct 2026 to 25 Mar 2027, Volcano Bay is closed for refurbishment. During this time, the ticket becomes a 3 Park Adventure Ticket.

SeaWorld Parks & Discovery Cove
Popular options include:

3‑Park Ticket – SeaWorld, Aquatica, Busch Gardens
14‑Day Unlimited Ticket – unlimited entry to all three
Discovery Cove – one‑day experience, can be bundled with 14‑day access to SeaWorld/Aquatica/Busch

Step 4 — Where to stay

Where to stay

Where you stay isn’t just “somewhere to sleep” — it completely shapes how your days feel.

Staying in the Disney area
Perfect if you want to be wrapped in Disney magic from morning to night. You get early entry, Disney transport, and the full “Disney bubble” feeling. Great for families and first‑timers.

Staying in the Universal area
Brilliant for teens, coaster lovers and Harry Potter fans. You get early entry, walkable access, water taxis, and Express Pass included at select hotels. Parking is not free — Universal hotel guests pay at the resort.

Staying on International Drive
Great for value, flexibility, restaurants, attractions and longer stays. Ideal if you want a bit of everything and don’t want to be tied to one resort bubble.

Staying in Lake Buena Vista
A lovely middle ground — close to Disney Springs with great hotel options and easy access to the parks.

The parks: what first‑timers really need to know

This is where the magic happens — and where the overwhelm usually starts. Let’s break it down simply.

Walt Disney World

Magic Kingdom
The classic. The castle. The fireworks. The nostalgia.

Don’t rush — soak it in
Save energy for the fireworks

EPCOT

My tips:
Guardians is unmissable

World Showcase is best enjoyed slowly

Don’t skip the shows

Go early
Bring water shoes
Use them as mid‑holiday reset days

Lightning Lanes (Multi, Single & Premier Pass)
Disney’s queue‑skipping system. There are different types of Lightning Lane passes, and how you use them depends on your plans, priorities and budget.

Universal Orlando

Universal Studios
Movies, Minions, Diagon Alley and a great mix of rides and shows.

My tips:
Take your time in Diagon Alley
Bourne Stuntacular is much better than you’d expect
Good balance for mixed‑age groups

Islands of Adventure
Coasters, Jurassic Park, Marvel and Seuss Landing.

My tips:
Hagrid’s is incredible
VelociCoaster is intense but amazing
Seuss Landing is lovely for little ones

Epic Universe
The brand‑new park that’s going to change how people plan their holidays.

My tips:
Give it a full day
Expect queues — it’s new and exciting
Onsite hotels with early entry will be a big help

Volcano Bay
A beautiful, tropical‑style water park with slides, a central volcano and plenty of space to relax.

My tips:
Arrive early for the most popular slides
Mix slides with chill time
Great mid‑holiday reset day

Express Pass
Universal’s queue‑skipping system. It can be included with certain hotels or bought separately.

SeaWorld, Aquatica & Busch Gardens

SeaWorld
Coasters, shows and animal encounters.

My tips:
Check showtimes early and plan around them
Mako is brilliant for thrill‑seekers
Animal exhibits are lovely for slower moments

Aquatica
Warm water, colourful slides and lazy rivers.

My tips:
Go early for the best chairs
Great for mixed ages
Nice balance of fun and relaxation

Busch Gardens
Big coasters plus wildlife. If it’s included in your ticket and you love thrills, it’s worth the trip.

Step 6 — Getting around

Getting around Orlando

Orlando looks huge on a map, and the first time you see the size of Disney alone you might think, “How are we getting anywhere without a helicopter?” But once you know the basics, it’s much easier than it seems.

Disney transport
If you’re staying in the Disney area, you’re basically in a bubble where everything is connected for you.

Buses
Monorail
Skyliner
Boats

It’s all free and runs constantly. Just give yourself time — it’s reliable, but it’s not teleportation.

Universal transport
Universal is much more compact than Disney, and that’s one of the reasons people love it.

Water taxis
Shuttle buses
Walking paths

Car hire vs ride‑share

Car hire is great if:
You want total flexibility
You’re staying offsite
You’re visiting Busch Gardens
You like doing big shopping trips

Uber/Lyft is great if:
You don’t want to drive abroad
You’re staying in Disney or Universal
You want to avoid parking fees
You’re mainly doing the main parks

Parking costs (current):
Disney: $32 per day (unless you’re staying at a Disney resort).
Universal: $32 per day (including hotel guests, who pay at the resort).

Step 7 — Dining

Dining in Orlando

Orlando is a dream for food lovers — and a bit of a minefield for first‑timers. There’s just so much choice. But don’t worry, I’ve got you.

Disney dining
Character meals – brilliant for families and a lovely way to meet characters without queueing
Table‑Service – great for a slower, more relaxed meal (some book up fast)
Quick‑Service – perfect for grabbing something tasty without losing park time

My tip: use mobile ordering where it’s available. It saves so much time and avoids the “hangry family meltdown” moment.

Universal dining
Universal is fantastic for food — especially around CityWalk. You’ll find big American classics, great quick‑service options, brilliant snacks and some surprisingly good sit‑down restaurants.

International Drive & Lake Buena Vista
If you’re staying offsite, you’ll be spoiled for choice. Everything from budget‑friendly chains to lovely local restaurants. On a longer stay, mixing parks, I‑Drive, Disney Springs and CityWalk keeps things fresh.

Step 8 — Budgeting

Budgeting for Orlando

Let’s be honest — Orlando isn’t cheap. But it is worth every penny when you plan it right.

Where the money goes
Flights
Hotel
Park tickets
Food
Transport
Extras (Lightning Lanes, Express Pass, souvenirs, etc.)

Money‑saving tips
Travel outside peak if you can
Stay offsite for more value
Bring snacks and refillable bottles
Use ride‑share instead of hiring a car for the whole trip
Plan your dining — impulse eating adds up fast

What to pack

Orlando weather has a mind of its own. One minute it’s blazing sunshine, the next it’s a five‑minute tropical downpour.

Essentials
Comfortable shoes
Ponchos
Portable chargers
Sun cream
Refillable water bottles
Cooling towels
Plasters

Things people forget
Swimwear for water rides and water parks
A lightweight jumper or cardigan for air‑con
A small park bag
Snacks for the kids

Step 10 — Final thoughts

Final thoughts

Here’s what I always tell my clients:

Orlando is magical — but it’s big, busy and full of moving parts. You are not expected to know everything, and you are definitely not expected to plan it all alone.

Pick your must‑dos. Take breaks. Drink water. Enjoy the moments that make you smile — the first time you see the castle, the kids’ faces on their favourite ride, the quiet evening by the pool.
